# Designer Principles — Talent Design

Binding rules, not suggestions. Two sources feed this file:

1. **Ported principles** — established through extensive spell-design sessions with the owner, translated to talent terms below. Each cites its spell-design provenance number; the full original reasoning lives in `.claude/skills/spell-design/references/principles/`.
2. **Talent-native principles** — distilled from owner feedback during talent work, appended over time (see "Appending a new principle" at the bottom).

Numbering is local to this file and stable — never renumber. The talent-specific structural rules (three progression paths, per-rank resource budget, rank progression pattern) live in [../SKILL.md](../SKILL.md); this file holds design-judgment rules.

## Shared spell-design principle files that bind talent text directly

Talent ability text follows the same house conventions as spell text. Before writing any talent, these spell-design files apply as-is (read "spell" as "talent ability"):

## Ported Principles

### Power budget

**1. Talents assist, never bypass.** A talent improves rolls, options, and economy inside a subsystem — it never auto-resolves a scene type or skips a game mode. An infiltration talent makes the infiltration challenge better, it does not replace the challenge with an auto-success. If a talent's best use is "we no longer play out X", redesign it. *(spell principle 1)*

**2. Flat damage riders don't proliferate.** The default talent damage rider is a small fixed ability bonus (+2 typical, per the §4.5 fixed-bonus table — never "+Skill Rank"). Only one ability bonus applies per hit, but every new rider still competes for that slot and adds mental load. New support, mark, and aura talents default to non-damage payoffs: action economy, a bane on the aggressor, a boon on attackers, free movement, condition control. Damage riders remain only where damage IS the talent's identity. *(spell principle 96; confirmed by the §4.2–4.3 talent damage audit)*

**3. Success Level manipulation is premium budget.** Upgrading a hit's Success Level by one step multiplies weapon damage — it compresses the entire damage curve into one ability. SL upgrades are R3+ material, carry a per-scene limit, and never stack with a flat damage rider inside the same talent (the Assassination R1+R3 lesson: SL upgrade plus flat rider produced near one-shots). *(adapted from spell principle 2 and the §4.3 audit)*

**4. Never scale a cap.** System caps are fixed guardrails, not scaling axes. Unarmored-defense AV never exceeds the armor it replaces ("AV = 1 + Fortitude (max 4)"), tier-gated effects never extend their tier reach with talent ranks, and no talent raises a cap another subsystem establishes. Scale honest axes instead: uses per scene, scope, reliability. *(spell principle 31; §4.6 AV caps)*

**5. A granted immunity needs a circumvention.** A talent granting immunity (to prone, push, fear, a damage type) must leave counterplay or it becomes an invisible invincibility wall. House pattern: acting aggressively or a specific circumstance degrades immunity to resistance or a boon, or the immunity carries a stance/equipment condition that enemies can see and attack. Flag for review: Polearm Mastery R3's unconditional prone/push immunity. *(spell principle 77)*

**6. Healing and recovery sit under the Life-tradition ceiling.** Talent healing must be strictly worse than a Life spell of comparable tier in at least one axis (cost, trigger narrowness, rate). Wound recovery never becomes routine through a talent. HP is encounter-scale only — it refills at any short break and never interacts with rest-scale effects. Rest acceleration shortens a rest, never banks an extra one, and no talent upgrades a night's rest into a full rest (full rest belongs to safe havens only). *(spell principles 67, 13, 16, 76, 81)*

**7. Fatigue-removal ceiling.** Nightly recovery baseline is 1 Fatigue; travel inflicts ~0–2 per day. A talent granting +1 removal already doubles recovery — that is the ceiling. Never scale removal further with ranks, or travel attrition stops threatening entirely. Directly binds every P2 travel-talent design. *(spell principle 17)*

### Triggers and conditions

**8. Debuffs key off rolls that actually happen.** The acting side rolls against static Defenses; a defender only rolls in specific carve-outs (end-of-turn saves, entering a hazard, contested checks). "The target suffers +1 bane to resist X" is dead text when X resolves by the attacker rolling. Grant the boon to rolls the party makes, lower a Defense, or apply a condition. *(spell principle 90)*

**9. High-impact conditions never ride automatic, no-roll triggers.** An on-miss or on-hit emitter uses a low-impact condition (distracted, briefly slowed); frightened and stronger need a roll, a save, or an SL gate. A defensive talent that frightens every attacker who whiffs is repeated hard control for free. *(spell principle 88)*

**10. Check every condition against its definition; parameterized conditions carry their (X).** Write "suffers burning (4) for a short duration", never "briefly burning". Copy a published talent's or spell's exact phrasing rather than writing from memory. *(spell principle 73)*

**11. Kill-riders say "slain", and never key on a condition the triggering attack consumes.** Elite and Lord creatures refill HP on Wounds, so "reduced to 0 HP" erases a boss mid-fight. Marked ends when the next attack resolves, so an attack cannot both consume and be gated by it. *(spell principle 80)*

### Scope and fiction

**12. Limits live in the fiction, not game structure.** Restrictions are diegetic: weapon type, armor class, stance, terrain, intoxication, daily rhythms. Never scope to meta-constructs ("only during encounter turns", "only in combat"). The per-rank resource budget (once per scene / once per day) is the sanctioned exception — it is the talent economy's pacing language, stated plainly. *(spell principle 12)*

**13. A flat effect on success is often correct, especially for utility.** Not every talent ability needs SL tiers or scaling. When the value is binary (you reroll or you don't, the contact exists or doesn't), one reliable effect on any success is a feature. Add tiers only where a genuine potency axis exists. *(spell principle 24)*

**14. GM-facing effects give the GM parameters, never open-ended "GM decides".** A talent that reveals information, reads intentions, or invokes contacts spells out a bounded menu of answer types the GM chooses among, with a safety-valve option when nothing concrete fits. Models: the *Magical Sense* talent, *Divination*. Critical for P2 designs: contact networks, informants, omen and prophecy talents. *(spell principle 29)*

**15. Weigh worldbuilding implications as an explicit balance axis.** Nexus is Bronze Age sword & sorcery — institutions are pre-modern and stay that way. Before finalizing a talent with social, economic, legal, or informational reach (forgery, contact networks, infiltration, truth-reading, wealth generation via crafting), ask what happens if it becomes a routine tool of courts, markets, armies, or guilds. Keep it rare or costly, prefer constrain-over-auto-solve, leave in-world counterplay, and add a short GM framing note where the reach is setting-shaping. *(spell principle 33)*

**16. The name is a promise, spoken in a pre-scientific register.** Every effect must fit what the name leads a player to expect, and the name must cover every mode. Names and flavor use Bronze Age vocabulary — no modern physics or science terms — but stay clear and functional first: function in the name, poetry in the flavor. Classic genre terms stay when recognizability wins. *(spell principles 61, 91)*

### Cross-system interfaces

**17. Cross-system interactions gate in the talent, never by disclaimers elsewhere.** A talent that modifies spells, items, or subsystem procedures carries every restriction in its own wording (the Communal Practices model: "a singular mystic spell that targets only a single willing creature"). Never scatter "this ignores talent X" text into spells or items. When designing a talent that touches another system, write the gate so future content in that system is automatically in or out by the talent's own words. *(spell principle 83)*

**18. Validate against spells, items, and subsystems — not just other talents.** Before finalizing, check whether a spell, folk trait, or item already covers the same job. A talent must not strictly outclass a same-job spell, and vice versa — put them in parity with distinct edges: the talent is repeatable, slot-bought, and upgrades with ranks; the spell costs Focus and needs no slot. Grep the spell files, not only the talent files. *(spell principle 27)*

## Talent-Native Principles

**19. High-rank abilities are never JUST a number bump.** Numbers do grow at R4–R5 — within the fixed bonus menus (analysis §4.5: damage {+2/+4/+6}, slow rank ceilings, +6 legal from R4). But a rank whose entire content is a numeric step reads boring and uninspired. Every R4–R5 rank leads with a new capability (scope expansion, action economy, reliability, system bridge, recovery); the menu step rides along as an accompaniment. The older phrasing "not bigger numbers at R4+" was an over-extension of the bounded-ceiling principle by the v2 analysis, corrected here. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-17, P0 review session: "of course numbers can grow at R4 and R5. But… these high rank abilities should not JUST scale numbers because that makes them boring and uninspired.")*

**20. Multi-ability ranks use the list format.** When one rank grants more than one individual ability, introduce them with "You gain the following abilities:" and list each on its own line with "- ". Never chain multiple abilities in flowing prose inside a rank, and never pack two abilities into one list item. Stance-conditional variants keep the same shape with the condition leading ("While enraged, you also gain the following effects:"). Single-ability ranks state the ability directly with no list. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-17, P0.2 review; live exemplars: Assassination R2, Martial Artist R2–R3, Battle Rage.)*

**21. The preamble holds only prerequisites and whole-talent conditions — never abilities — and gateway talents need no preamble at all.** Text before the first "(Rank 1)" block is reserved for general gates: skill prerequisites ("Requires Artisan Rank 3"), equipment restrictions across independent abilities ("You can't use any of these abilities while wearing heavy armor.", Escape Artist model), or exclusivity lines. Every ability, including a stance's entry/exit/lifecycle mechanics, lives inside a rank block — a stance's lifecycle is its R1 unlock content. Refinement: when every rank of the talent flows through one gateway ability (a stance like Battle Rage, where R2+ all nest under "while enraged"), attach the condition to the gateway ability itself in Rank 1 ("While you wear no heavy armor, you can enter…") — gating the entry gates the whole talent, no preamble needed. *(Owner rulings 2026-07-17, P0.2 review: first correcting a Battle Rage draft that put the rage lifecycle in the preamble, then folding the armor condition into R1 as well.)*

**22. Bonuses are typed by source lane — the two-lane rider rule.** Four canonical types (published in `docs/01-basic-rules/05-general-rulings.md`): **ability** (your own talents, folk abilities, innate — standing self-riders), **situational** (external or momentary: another creature's aid, positioning, terrain, triggered grants), **enhancement** (improvements attached to an item, the item's own stats), **armor** (armor worn or an armor stand-in, AV only). Same type never stacks (highest wins), different types stack, untyped stacks. A character's own standing riders live in the ability lane; buffs granted to them from outside live in the situational lane — your training and their blessing stack, two blessings don't. Boons and banes are never typed. AV never routes through ability. Permanent build gains (+2 HP, +2 Focus) stay untyped. One-roll effects (combat arts) may stay untyped. Spells reuse these types by what the effect does — there is no "spell bonus" type; a spell is a delivery mechanism, not a source lane. *(Owner rulings 2026-07-17, P0.4: taxonomy triggered by Inspire Ally's ability-typed grant being dead against allies' own passive riders; "situational" confirmed over "support"; no spell-bonus type confirmed.)*

**23. Bonus values come from fixed magnitude menus.** Damage riders from **{+2, +4, +6}** with slow rank ceilings: R1 +2 exceptional (rolled or tightly limited activations only), R2 +2 normal, R3–R4 +4, +6 legal from R4 (exceptional) and broadly at R5. Healing riders run one step ahead (R1 +2, R2 +4, R3 +6); whole-ability heals (Second Wind model — the heal is the entire costed ability) ladder 4/6/8 across talent ranks. Defense bonuses (Parry/Dodge/Resist/Defenses) from **{+1, +2}**, +2 the ceiling. Pools (temp HP and absorption barriers) from **{5, 10, 20}**: 5 = R2–R3, 10 = R4–R5 or heavily restricted, 20 = R5/legendary. Marked (X) draws from the damage menu. No odd damage values. Riders entering at R1/R2 of a multi-rank talent grow via an in-talent ladder ("increases to +4" at the talent's R3) — the talent's own ranks carry the growth the old +Skill formulas provided implicitly. Exempt: talents where rank-up IS the identity (Spell Knowledge Focus ladder, Pugilist) and cast-parameter values set by the spell being cast (Mana Shield's 2×rank barrier). Full tables: TALENT_SYSTEM_ANALYSIS §4.5. *(Owner rulings 2026-07-17, P0.2/P0.3: even menu only, slow ceilings, pool steps mirroring creature HP brackets, whole-ability heal ladder, in-talent ladders applied.)*

**24. Basic talents can grow R4–R5 organically — without becoming Signature talents.** When a Basic talent's identity naturally continues past R3 (Maintenance's repair ladder gaining broken-item recovery), extend it in place rather than spinning up a separate High-Level talent or adding it to the Signature list. The closed Signature list of 11 stands; an organic extension carries no Signature designation and no new path category — it is the same Basic talent with more rungs. Prefer this when the new ranks deepen the talent's existing job; use a separate High-Level talent (Master Artisan model) when the prestige ability is a new job that would bloat its host, and never overload a rank to avoid the question — one rank, one or two effects, move the overflow to where it thematically lives. *(Owner rulings 2026-07-18, Master Artisan/Maintenance session: rejected folding R4–R5 into Artisan for Master Artisan's new-capability set, then ruled broken-item repair moves to Maintenance R4–R5 as "just organic extension. no signature talent.")*

**25. The mortal-pinnacle bar is "powered by the mortal self," not "realistic."** High-rank talents (especially R5) may be supernatural *from a realistic point of view* — wall-running across a waterfall, a conqueror's roar that staggers a warband, instinctively re-reading a moment before it lands — and may even draw on flatly unrealistic power, as long as the source is the character's own mortal self: body, will, authority, instinct, or soul. The published precedent is Supreme Combat Arts (`docs/05-combat/05-combat-arts/00-overview.md`): they "transcend mere physical ability and tap into the very power of a warrior's soul" and are still fully mortal in-world. What crosses the line is *external* or *magical* power: Focus costs, spell effects, divine or spirit patronage, reality-warping, or effects only magic could explain in the fiction (flight, true precognition answering questions, permanent aura upkeep). That normal people in-world perceive a Grandmaster as supernatural is expected framing, not a design constraint — the same way elite athletes read as superhuman in the real world. The wuxia/mythic-hero register is the intended feel of the Grandmaster tier, not a violation of it. Bounded delivery still applies: rolled contests for high-impact conditions, once-per-day for session-defining moments. *(Owner rulings 2026-07-18, P1.3 session: "supernatural from a realistic point of view but still the mortal pinnacle in-world," then refined with the Supreme Combat Arts soul-power precedent — soul-channeling is unrealistic yet totally "mortal" in-world; outside perception of supernatural is just framing.)*

**26. Condition severity comes from the Nexus definitions, not genre assumptions.** Never reason about a condition from its name or its D&D counterpart — open `docs/05-combat/04-conditions.md` and design against what the condition actually does. The canonical trap: Nexus **stunned** does not disable a creature (they still move or act, at +1 bane, no Quick Actions); full incapacitation is **paralyzed** alone. Consequences: stunned is a legitimate rolled-AoE ceiling condition rather than an encounter-deleting one, while paralyzed sits a full severity tier higher and needs correspondingly harsher gates (rolled, single-target or very limited, repeat-save escape valves as in Paralyzing Poison and Sacred Covenant). Budget and drama assessments (principle 9, R4–R5 ceilings) key off the real text. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P1.3 session: corrected an overestimation of Presence of Conquest R5's stun-on-critical — "stunned doesnt fully disable a creature in Nexus RPG, that is only achieved by paralyzing them.")*

**27. System vocabulary is a payoff, not a gate.** A talent may resolve its effect using a subsystem's defined terms (Motivation, Pitfall, Interest, Patience) without narrowing its *trigger* to only fire while that subsystem's formal challenge is running. An ability that reveals a Motivation or Pitfall works before any meaningful interaction with the NPC in question — every NPC carries those GM-facing notes regardless of whether the scene ever escalates into a declared Social Intrigue. Reserve the tighter "only during an active challenge" gate for abilities that manipulate a challenge's own live, numeric resources (Interest, Patience, the challenge die itself) — those genuinely don't exist until an instance is running. Broad-trigger, system-flavored payoff describes more of the game than narrow-trigger, system-exclusive payoff. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P2 batch 1 review: "you can generally collect information about a person before interacting with them this way to learn something useful about them, using the framework of Social Intrigue to codify how you collect information e.g. uncovering a Motivation or Pitfall." Applied to City's Pulse R2; carries forward to Read the Room and any future Motivation/Pitfall-reveal design.)*

**28. Challenges are a resolution overlay, not a dependency.** A Challenge is a mechanical framework the GM lays over a scene to keep dramatic pacing procedural — it is not itself the fiction, and an ability should never need one running to function. Describe the talent's trigger as a narrative action first (leaning on a contact, reading a room, tracking a trail); give that action one baseline payoff that works in any scene, structured or freeform. Only then layer a Challenge-specific effect (typically challenge-die manipulation) as an *alternate* resolution of the same action, gated by whether the Challenge's pace actually admits it — legwork like calling in a favor fits a Research or Investigation's timescale, not a Chase's. Never write the ability's sole effect as "reduce the challenge die," which makes the ability inert outside a declared Challenge and inverts cause and effect (the mechanic should follow the fiction, not gate it). *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P2 batch 1 review, City's Pulse R3: "Challenges can be all sorts of scenes, they are again just a mechanical framework to resolve scenes dramatically and procedurally to keep the pacing up. Abilities should not rely on this structure to work, it should be the other way around." Also requires respecting each Challenge's pace — a favor-calling ability that fits Research/Investigation doesn't fit a Chase.)*

**29. Ground a genuine narrative-fact term with concrete examples the first time a talent names it.** Capitalized nouns that describe a real property of a person or scene (Motivation, Pitfall) read as bare jargon to a player who hasn't internalized the subsystem. The first time a talent's ability text names one, an orienting opener plus that term's own defining examples helps ("During a conversation that matters to the NPC, appealing to a Motivation — a desire, fear, bond, or value — raises their Interest"). This pairs with principle 27: these terms earn their capital letter because the fiction genuinely contains them regardless of which subsystem is running. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P2 batch 1 review, Road Warden R1: "it needs to introduce with something like 'during travel when the party reaches a safepoint, such as an inn, other, or other, you can roll...'" — the underlying lesson generalized, then split from principle 30 once the Road Warden example itself turned out to belong to the opposite case: a procedural bookkeeping label that should shed its capital letter rather than be grounded with examples.)*

**30. Procedural bookkeeping labels get natural language, not a capital letter.** Safepoint, Blockade, Landmark, Camp, Encounter, Weather Event, Wear and Tear — these are the GM's own table-category tags for organizing checkpoints and random events, not facts the fiction contains. A table running a scene in freeform roleplay, without ever consulting the travel rules' tables, would say "we stopped at the inn" or "bandits jumped us on the road," never "we reached a Safepoint" or "an Encounter happened." Write talent triggers in that same plain register — "a safe waypoint, such as an inn, farmstead, or friendly settlement," "a hostile encounter," "a blockade in the road" — so the ability reads the same whether the table is running the procedure literally or improvising around it; the natural phrasing still maps onto the exact same procedural spot for a group using the tables. Contrast with principle 27/29's Motivation, Pitfall, Interest, Patience, which stay capitalized because they name something the fiction actually has, not a GM's filing category. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P2 batch 1 review, Road Warden: "you are capitalizing terms too much like Safepoint or Encounter. again, natural language over game terms for these systems which can either be run strictly by the procedures of the game or free-flowing in roleplay.")*

**31. "Unprovoked" attaches to the move it describes — don't split it into its own sentence.** When a talent grants a single specific move ("you can immediately move a close distance"), fold the keyword directly onto that clause — "you can immediately move a close distance unprovoked" — matching the published Fast Stride R3 pattern ("immediately move a close or short distance unprovoked"). A trailing "This Movement is unprovoked." sentence says the same thing in twice the words. Reserve the standalone-sentence form for when the grant is a standing passive not tied to one specific move clause, as in the published Escape Artist R3 exemplar ("Once between your turns, your Movement is unprovoked") — there the sentence describes a general state covering whatever Movement happens on that turn, not a single move being granted in the same breath. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P2 batch 1/5 review, Shadow Slip R2 and Desperate Gambit R2: "'you can immediately move a close distance. This Movement is unprovoked.' is too wordy. other abilities simplify this by just saying '... move a close distance unprovoked.'")*

**32. A single ability should basically never impose +2 banes.** Multiple banes are a much bigger swing than they look — per the actual boon/bane math (`docs/01-basic-rules/01-how-to-roll.md`), the first bane rolls 2d6 take-lower (~-1 average), but the second adds a further flat -1 on top, so +2 banes lands close to a flat -2 to the roll with cut-off upside on top — harsh enough that it reads as a control effect, not a minor debuff, and belongs to conditions and dedicated control talents, not a routine reactive-defense or crafting-tier rider. When a design's numbers seem to call for +2 banes to reach parity with a peer ability, that is a signal the whole approach is wrong, not that the bane count should go up: match the peer's actual mechanic instead (a flat Defense bonus, for instance — Shadow Slip R1 was corrected from +2 banes to the same +2 targeted Defense its peers use), or spread the difficulty across a different axis entirely (time, coin, scope) the way Forger R1's tier ladder was corrected to escalate via exploration turns instead of stacking a second bane. If a talent's own ranks can combine to expose +2 banes on one roll in the worst case (a tier bane plus a rush-option bane, say), an unconditional same-rank boon must always apply alongside it so the net a player actually rolls never exceeds +1. This ceiling targets a *raw, uncontested* +2 modifier landing on one roll — it does not forbid a talent's own boon and bane sources coexisting on the same roll and netting to something smaller (Forger's rush bane plus tier bane, offset by R3's standing +1 boon, nets to +1 at worst): that's the mechanic's own escape valve working as designed, confirmed as the intended exception rather than a violation. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-18, P2 batch 1 review, Shadow Slip R1 and then Forger R1: "+2 banes should basically never be imposed from a single ability. Find another way for the ability to work which is powerful enough" — caught a second instance in Forger's noble/royal tier during the same review, confirming this is a general ceiling, not a one-off correction. Owner then flagged Forger's own offsetting boon/bane combination as "an exception I think," confirmed here.)*

**33. High-cooldown ability, earned recharge — a payoff pattern to reach for deliberately, not stumble into.** A rank that grants a rare, powerful resource (once per adventure, once per day) can make a *later* rank's payoff the ability to earn that resource back early, tied to a fitting in-fiction trigger, instead of bolting on an unrelated new effect. Pathfinder was the original model: R1 grants a once-per-adventure Navigation re-roll; R2's payoff is "passing a notable landmark lets you use it again" — the landmark is diegetic (principle 12), the recharge deepens R1's own resource rather than adding new surface area, and R2 genuinely earns its "flourish that rewards commitment" job description instead of being a generic bonus. The corpus has thin precedent for this at the *scarce, campaign-scoped* tier — Piercing Look R3 and Identify Weakness R2 recharge a once-per-scene ability on a kill, which is the same shape but far cheaper and more frequent. Reach for the rare-resource version deliberately when a rank feels like a weak bonus in isolation: check whether an earlier rank in the same talent already grants something scarce, and whether the new rank's fictional trigger (a checkpoint, a kill, a milestone) can plausibly refill it instead of adding beside it. *(Owner query 2026-07-18, P2 batch 2 review, after the Pathfinder R2 fix: "we generally have almost no interaction like this in talents, no? I would like to introduce some more of these 'high cooldown ability' + 'early cooldown reset' synergies. where should we put this in the roadmap?" — answered: apply opportunistically for the remainder of this P2 pass; flag for the P3 signature re-audit, since several of the 11 signatures already sit on once-per-day/once-per-scene resources with natural recharge anchors; and add as a named P4 R4–R5 template alongside the existing five, see SKILL.md.)* **Update 2026-07-19:** Pathfinder itself was later rebuilt for multi-system spread (principle 35) and no longer carries the once-per-adventure re-roll plus landmark recharge. The pattern still stands as documented and is earmarked for deliberate P4 use, it just no longer has a live in-batch exemplar.

**34. No em/en dashes or semicolons in any game-facing talent text.** CLAUDE.md bans these in published game content, and that ban is absolute for every word a player or GM will read: the ability rows themselves and any draft text destined to become the published talent. Split into two sentences, or use "such as" / "and", the same fixes CLAUDE.md prescribes. Check every draft ability row for both marks before moving on, the same way capitalization and gating get checked, since the draft row is copied verbatim to the published surfaces. Rationale, budget, and scope prose in the draft file is meta-commentary and falls under CLAUDE.md's meta-doc exemption, so it is not held to this rule, but the ability text must be clean the moment it is written. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 review, Dogged Pursuit R1: "you used '—'. the principles should include not using any dashes like this or semicolon in any texts." — the '—' in question was in the R1 ability text itself; scoped to game-facing text to match CLAUDE.md's meta-doc carve-out.)*

**35. A talent must earn its slot across multiple game systems, not just one low-screen-time system.** A good talent integrates useful abilities for more than one game mode. The load-bearing exception is combat, which is a large enough share of play that a purely combat talent is fine on its own. Everything else, and especially travel, needs company: a travel sequence pops up between adventures with far less screen time than dungeon delving or combat, so a talent whose entire kit fires only during travel (or only during any single narrow subsystem) is a weak buy most builds skip. The published pools already enforce this and are the reference: Athletics and Education have zero travel-only talents; Nature has exactly one (Knowledgeable Wanderer), which pads every rank with +2 HP to be worth taking; and even Survival, the skill that owns travel and exploration, gives every talent a combat reach (Monster Hunter is pure combat, Relentless Tracker ends in an ambush, Trap Maker is a dungeon-and-combat tool, Wilderness Expert wakes you before an attack). The house idiom for a utility skill: **R1** a cheap re-roll or unlock on the skill's core out-of-combat use, **R2** a combat or dungeon application that turns the expertise into a field edge (Empath, Intuitive Appraisal, Piercing Look, Diplomat's Demoralize, Monster Hunter all do this at R2), **R3** mastery. When designing a talent for an out-of-combat theme (travel, research, social, crafting), deliberately give at least one rank a dungeon-delving or combat application, and keep the narrow-system ability as one facet of the kit rather than its whole. Anchor each added application to a real published mechanic in that system, and run the overlap audit against the skill's own pool so the new reach doesn't duplicate an existing talent's niche. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 review: "the main issue is that they all only produce abilities for the travel system... A good talent integrates useful abilities for multiple game systems. An exception are combat talents of course, because combat encounters are a large part of the game. but a travel sequence will only pop up in between adventures with much less screen time... Existing talents for these support/utility skills already tried at this design challenge." Drove the full rebuild of P2 batch 2, where all five travel-only talents were re-spread across travel plus dungeon delving and/or combat.)*

**36. Every ability states how it resolves, and does not simply switch a mechanic off.** Two linked faults, both caught on Pathfinder R2/R3. First, an ability must carry enough mechanical context to run at the table: what you roll (Attribute + Skill), against what (a Defense, a Resist, a named or GM-set difficulty), and what each outcome does. "You can find a route that bypasses one natural obstacle" and "you can approach a location unseen" name an effect but no resolution, leaving the GM to invent the whole procedure. Anchor the trigger and resolution to a real published mechanic instead: the Event Die and dice timer (`docs/06-scenes/01-scenes-time-intervals.md`) for delve/exploration/travel events, the surprise rolls for ambushes, the Navigator bonus list for travel pace, a bounded GM-answer menu (principle 14) for information. Second, prefer a rolled, failable resolution over an ability that removes a resolution entirely. "You retrace a route without a roll" or "you always know the way back" reads as switching a mechanic off (a cousin of principle 1's bypass ban, applied at the single-ability scale, not just the scene scale): the fix is to make the mastery a real roll that returns a concrete result, such as a delving-turn bearings roll vs. a GM difficulty that answers one bounded orientation question. Established defined passives with strong precedent are the exception, not a violation: "ignore difficult terrain" (Athletic Movement R2, Bulky R2) is a specific, bounded movement modifier the game already grants, not an ungrounded auto-bypass. The test: does the ability tell you what to roll and what happens, and does it resolve something rather than delete it? *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 Pathfinder review: "both dont provide enough mechanical context for how to resolve them. Also, I dont like 'you can just ignore mechanics' abilities like with the first example [retrace a route without a roll].")*

**37. No phantom equipment, and no generic omniscient-intel rolls.** Two rejected shapes from Expedition Leader, both failed attempts at a cross-system reach (principle 35). First, phantom equipment: an ability that lets a character retroactively "have packed" or conjure a piece of gear they never recorded carrying (the torch you didn't note, the rope you need at the chasm) does not work, even bounded by a GM veto. Equipment is tracked, bought, and carried, so an ability that produces it from nothing sidesteps the whole inventory and encumbrance economy and reads as a cheat rather than a skill. If a logistics talent wants to matter in the field, anchor it to a real resource mechanic the party already tracks (Supply checks, uses, the Resource Drain event), where the character optimizes or protects resources that genuinely exist, rather than inventing new ones mid-scene. Second, generic omniscient intel: an ability that reads "roll [skill] vs. a GM difficulty, learn the layout / a hazard / a notable feature" of any site is too generic (it works everywhere, on anything, and is barely more than rolling the base skill) and too potent (it front-loads the answers a scene is built to reveal through play). If a talent grants information, tie it to a specific trigger (a thing discovered, a creature encountered), give it a mechanical edge beyond the base skill (a re-roll, a boon, a guaranteed partial), and keep the answer a tightly bounded single item (principle 14), not a full briefing. *(Owner rulings 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 Expedition Leader review: "this kind of phantom equipment doesnt really work" and "the second rank 1 ability... is also too generic and potent." Rebuilt around Supply checks and the Resource Drain event instead.)*

**38. Keep flavor out of the mechanical clauses.** Ability text should read as mechanics stated plainly, not mechanics wrapped in narrative color. Phrases like "your careful provisioning covers the loss," "your foresight covers the shortfall," "you anticipated it and," or listing "ammunition scattering after a fight, a light source guttering in the dark" mid-sentence bloat the rule and make it slower to parse at the table. State the trigger, the roll, and the effect directly ("Once per day, when you or an ally within close range fails a Supply check, no use is spent"), and compress any needed examples to a bare list ("ammunition, light, or rations"). A talent's flavor lives in its name and, if anywhere, in a short framing lead, never braided through the resolution. This is the same discipline behind the earlier "sounds gamified" and "narrative vignette" corrections, applied to the opposite failure mode: not too gamey, but too flowery. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 Expedition Leader review: "too much flavor in the middle of mechanical descriptions.")*

**39. Upgrade an ability with an added effect, not a lower TN, and gate the addition on success level.** When a later rank deepens an earlier rank's rolled ability, do not upgrade it by reducing its difficulty (hard to medium). Lowering the TN is a flat success-chance bump that adds no new capability and reads as a bare number tweak (the cousin of principle 19's "not just a number"), and a rank that adds a positive rider is already raising the ability's value, so the difficulty change is redundant on top. Instead, keep the roll's difficulty and attach the new benefit to a strong or critical success, so the upgrade rewards rolling well and rides the Success Level system the game already uses. Pattern: R2 "on a success, X"; R3 "and on a strong or critical success, also Y." *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 Expedition Leader R3: "You improve your success chance either way so reducing the Difficulty is not necessary. And the extra effect should not apply always, only on a strong or critical success maybe?")*

**40. Cross-reference another rank's ability by name, not by a bare "(Rank X)".** When one rank refers to an ability granted at an earlier rank, name it the way the published talents do ("your Monster Hunter ability", "your fleeting ward", "your protective blessing"), not with a bare rank pointer like "(Rank 2)". If the earlier ability has no short name of its own, refer to it as "your [Talent Name] ability" ("with your Expedition Leader ability"). If the talent has two distinct sub-abilities that must be told apart, give each a name and use those ("your protective blessing" for R1, "your warding blessing" for R2) rather than "your Rank 1 blessing". The rank number is bookkeeping, not fiction, and a player reads the ability by its name at the table. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 batch 2 Expedition Leader R3: "replace the '(Rank 2)' with 'with your Expedition Leader ability'." Applied across the drafts, including Arcane Warding and Guardian's Oath in batch 5.)*

**41. Write fixed difficulties in TN notation.** When an ability rolls against a fixed, named difficulty, write it as "vs. TN X" (for example "roll Mind + Education vs. TN 10"), not "vs. a hard difficulty", "a hard Mind + Education test", or "hard Difficulty (10)". The ladder (`docs/01-basic-rules/01-how-to-roll.md`): Trivial 4, Easy 6, Medium 8, Hard 10, Very Hard 12. TN notation is shorter and unambiguous, and it is the owner's preferred style going forward. Variable or contested targets keep their natural form and take no TN number, since there is no fixed value to state: "vs. their Resist", "vs. the source's Parry", "vs. the travel difficulty", "vs. a difficulty the GM sets". When a talent lists a difficulty ladder (a tiered TN by target), write each rung as a TN too ("TN 8 for common folk, TN 10 for notable figures, TN 12 for rulers"). *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Expedition Leader review: "'you can roll Mind + Education vs. a hard difficulty' is clunky wording... I want to use the TN-notation more in the future. so 'roll Mind + Education vs. TN 10' in this case.")*

**42. A consumable benefit must spend a tracked resource.** An ability that produces or grants something consumable, a meal, provisions, a prepared aid, a restorative draught, must debit uses of a real, tracked item and name the supply it draws on. Otherwise a "once per day" or per-scene limit alone lets the benefit appear from nowhere, the consumable cousin of phantom equipment (principle 37). The travel roles produce perishable provisions the design can reference directly: the Forager yields edible plants, the Hunter raw meat, the Fisher fish, all rations-type supplies carried with a Supply die (`docs/06-scenes/07-challenges/02-travel.md`). So Seasoned Forager's restorative meal spends 1 use of the party's foraged provisions for every two who share it rather than costing nothing, scaling the cost to how many are fed rather than a flat charge. The valid alternative, when the ability gathers its raw materials from the environment as part of the action rather than drawing on carried supply, is to gate on time and circumstance instead (Makeshift Artisan spends a use of carried materials, while Seasoned Forager's improvised aids spend a delving turn of on-the-spot gathering, capped by Nature) but a repeatable benefit that draws on existing stores must debit those stores. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Seasoned Forager R3: "is missing mechanical instructions like using up uses of some kind of supply. The travel action gives you perishable supply items, so you can reference those.")*

**43. A challenge info-reveal pairs a vague description with a terse bracketed effect.** When a talent lets the GM reveal "one useful detail" during a challenge, do not over-specify the content of the reveal. Grounding each option in a concrete challenge element (a not-yet-triggered end consequence, an effective untried skill) reads as clunky and forces the GM to invent facts on the spot. Instead keep the narrative description vague and evocative, and attach a short mechanical effect in brackets that states what it does in the challenge structure: "an obstacle ahead (ignore the consequence of your next failure)", "an opening to press (+1 boon on your next roll)", "a misstep to catch (remove one pending consequence)". The bracket makes the reveal actionable without scripting the fiction, and the player picks which effect they want. Keep these effects below the talent's premium rank: soft boons and single-consequence mitigation at the payoff rank (R2), reserving challenge-die manipulation for the mastery rank (R3). This is the challenge-facing companion to principle 14 (bounded GM parameters) and principle 28 (challenges are an overlay). *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Keen Observer R2: "you can leave the descriptions more vague. but add small additions with what this achieves within the Challenge structure '(ignore next failure)' for example.")*

**44. Balance a physical skill's armor-restricted talents, don't pile onto one side.** Before adding a talent to a physical skill (Athletics above all), check the pool's spread of armor gates. Athletics carried five light-armor or no-heavy-armor talents (Escape Artist, Evasion, Fast Stride, Supernatural Mobility, Light Armor Mastery) and nothing a heavily armored character wants, so a new no-heavy-armor talent (Desperate Gambit) made the imbalance worse and left plate-wearers with almost no Athletics options. Prefer an armor-agnostic design (no gate) so neither side is shut out, or design deliberately for the under-served side. When choosing which side, remember that some armor-mastery lanes already live in other skills (Fortitude owns Heavy Armor Mastery), so do not clone those, an armor-agnostic talent any build can take is the safe default. Bull Rush (armor-agnostic forced movement) replaced Desperate Gambit for exactly this reason, and along the way dropped a damage-halving effect that duplicated Evasion R3 in the same pool. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Bull Rush review: "athletics needs to provide talents for both lightly armored AND heavily armored character. Currently, I think the light armor support is much stronger" and "they dont have to be explicit heavy-armor talents... talents that dont discriminate between worn armor would also be fine.")*

**45. Check the Combat Arts too, and design combat-maneuver talents to synergize with them, not duplicate them.** Before finalizing any combat-maneuver talent, read the Combat Arts (`docs/05-combat/05-combat-arts/`) alongside the talent pool, spells, and items. The Combat Arts own a large swath of maneuver design space: Charge and Ram Down (move plus Shove plus follow), Felling Strike (Trip), Snare (grapple plus restrain), Knockout (daze or stun). A talent that re-creates one of these is redundant, since a character can simply take the art. Design the talent as a force-multiplier for the existing Actions and Arts instead, building a combined playstyle: enhance the Shove and Trip Actions and the shove/trip Combat Arts (bigger push distance, larger valid targets, collision knockdown, an area burst) rather than granting a parallel copy of them. This is the Combat-Arts-facing companion to principle 18 (validate against spells and items) and shares the mindfulness of principle 15's reach check. It constrains execution, not direction. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Bull Rush review: the first Bull Rush granted a charge-into-free-Shove, duplicating the Charge and Ram Down Combat Arts. Owner: "make sure that these talents synergize with other things like combat arts for a combined playstyle and also dont infringe on existing mechanics of other talents. thats just a mindfulness problem on your end no the design direction being wrong." Reworked into an enhancer of the Shove/Trip Actions and the shove Combat Arts.)*

**46. The arcane defensive lanes are mostly taken, so anti-magic talents disrupt, they do not re-mitigate.** Before designing an Arcana defensive or anti-caster talent, map the ecosystem that already exists: Battle Mage R1 gives a Quick-Action, Focus-fuelled, repeatable reactive +2 to Parry/Dodge against weapon attacks; every arcane discipline carries a reactive rank-1 spell that grants +2 to a Defense against an incoming attack (plus a discipline rider); Mana Shield builds absorption barriers from casting; the Counterspell spell (Telepathy) hard-nullifies an enemy cast; and Mage Hunter (Lore) is the Focus-free martial anti-caster. Reactive-defense and damage-absorption are therefore covered. A new Arcana anti-magic talent should lead with disruption and spell-dueling rather than more mitigation: impose banes on enemy casting (a pseudo-counterspell short of the Counterspell spell's full nullify), drain a foiled caster's Focus, and stack with counterspelling and with the reactive defenses. When such a talent does need a reactive-defense component, build it as the Resist-side counterpart to Battle Mage R1 using the identical mechanism (Quick Action plus Focus for +2, then repeatable per incoming effect for the Focus alone), so it composes with Battle Mage across attack types instead of overlapping it. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Arcane Warding review: "Look at Battle Mage R1... Each arcane discipline also has a reactive R1 spell... the focus should be less about damage mitigation (Mana Shield already exists) and be more about pseudo-counterspell abilities and stacking on stuff." Rebuilt Arcane Warding into Spellbreaker: R1 mirrors Battle Mage R1 for Resist, R2 disrupts casting at medium range, R3 is a once-per-scene rolled pseudo-counterspell. Note: a Focus-drain effect was rejected because NPC casters run on prepared spells with no Focus pool, so Focus-drain does nothing against most enemies.)*

**47. When a talent's effect depends on vague, untracked prerequisite state, abstract it into a meta-currency.** If a talent reads "when you have researched / prepared / studied this" and a player would have to self-track that state to use it, replace the bookkeeping with a resource pool modeled on Divine Favor (Mysticism): a pool gained by defined actions at defined rates (the potent source scaling 1/2/3 by success level, a weaker source giving a flat point), spent on a menu of effects that grows across ranks, with a cap and a spending cadence ("spending requires no action, but only once between your turns"). The currency itself becomes the abstraction of the prerequisite, so no topic or target tracking is needed, and the resource decisions make the talent more interesting than a set of static conditionals. Methodical Research's Research pool is the second instance of this pattern (gained from the downtime Research activity and Bundle of Scrolls, spent on knowledge boons, skill-lending, and a field payoff). Keep each such currency distinct from Divine Favor and from each other in skill, gain source, and spend menu, so they read as parallel systems rather than reskins. *(Owner ruling 2026-07-19, P2 Methodical Research review: "how can the player say 'I researched that topic'. do they have to actually keep track? Can we introduce a 'Research' meta currency for the talent which behaves similar to Divine Favor where you can cash in points... make it more interesting and exciting.")*

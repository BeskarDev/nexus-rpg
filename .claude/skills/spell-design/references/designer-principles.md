## Full Principles

When the owner corrects or refines a design decision in session, append it here (numbered, one line, with the reasoning) **and add its hook to the Index by Concern above**.

1. **Spells assist, never bypass** — travel/social/exploration spells grant boons and reduce difficulty; they never auto-succeed skill challenges or trivialize game modes.
2. **SL scales magnitude, not effect type** — the primary utility/control effect (condition, duration, target count) is identical on any success. Only damage, healing, and *secondary* effects scale with Success Level. A control spell applying "dazed" on Weak still applies "dazed" on Critical — the Critical adds bonus damage or a secondary debuff.
3. **Heighten, don't duplicate** — reserve new spell entries for genuinely different concepts.
4. **Standardized reactive defense** — every school's R1 Quick Action reactive spell has the same base ("+2 to Dodge or Parry against the triggering attack"), no SL scaling, plus one school-specific secondary effect.
5. **R5 material costs match the item economy** — 5,000+ coins (Q6–Q7 tier), never trivial amounts.
6. **Traits are thematic seeds, not literal requirements** — an R0 "teleportation" cantrip means spatial manipulation (interact at range, sense space), not actual teleportation. Interpret traits creatively at each power level.
7. **Arcane defiles, Mystic honors** — even on shared thematic territory (Necromancy vs. Death), philosophies are opposed. Never share spells between philosophically opposed schools. (Genuinely neutral spells between compatible schools can be shared — see per-school notes, e.g. Acid Splash between Evocation and Nature.)
8. **Binding means containment** — Conjuration's binding trait is lasting constructs (wards, cages, dimensional anchors), not movement debuffs.
9. **Every school needs internal completeness** — R1 reactive Quick Action spell, defensive options, utility/non-combat effects, some damage capability, a repeating condition/gimmick identity, and setup+payoff synergies.
10. **AoE trades damage for targets** — half single-target spell bonus at R2+ (R0–R1 keep legacy values). AoE pays off at 3+ clustered targets (150% throughput), never strictly beats focused fire.
11. **No R0 Quick Action healing** — Quick Action healing starts at R1. At-will, action-free healing would trivialize attrition; healing always costs Focus or an Action.
12. **Limits must live in the fiction, never in game structure** — never scope an effect to meta-constructs ("only during encounter turns", "only in combat"). Constrain power through diegetic costs the world can see: concentration, Focus, Actions, duration, positioning, material costs, or daily rhythms. If an effect is too strong outside combat, change its magnitude or duration — don't bolt on a combat-only gate.
13. **Wound healing gates at R4+ for new spells** — Wounds are serious injuries; recovering them magically is a major effect. Existing lower-rank exceptions (Healing Touch R1 ritual-only, Abundance of Life R3) are grandfathered because their restrictions are intense (extended casting / once per day per creature + Action cost). Don't add new wound healing below R4.
14. **Shared rules must be visible at the point of use — never buried in reference pages** — a global rule hidden in a rules chapter gets forgotten at the table. Default for a shared limiting mechanism: repeat the **same short sentence verbatim** in every affected spell (current example: "A creature can only benefit from one Wound-healing spell effect per day."). A **spell property keyword** (the *illusory* model) is the escalation path only when the family grows large enough that verbatim repetition becomes a maintenance burden — and note a property still hides the rule one lookup away, so the bar is high (owner call).
15. **Effect text order: general application first, exceptions after** — declare the basic effect completely, then follow with its restrictions/exceptions as separate sentences. Never interleave a limit into the middle of the effect declaration.
16. **HP is encounter-scale only** — HP refills at any short break; it never interacts with rest-scale or travel-scale effects. Night/travel effects trade in the long-term trackers: Fatigue, Wounds, Resolve, Focus. "Regain HP at dawn" is a nonsense effect.
17. **Fatigue removal must respect travel attrition** — nightly recovery baseline is 1 Fatigue; travel inflicts ~0–2/day. A spell granting +1 removal already doubles recovery (acceptable ceiling); never scale removal higher via Heighten or rank, or fatigue accumulation stops threatening entirely.
18. **Heighten upscales, never transforms** — Heighten entries may only increase existing magnitudes (damage, healing, target count, range, duration, reach/timeframe). A genuinely new capability (new mode of use, new sense, new interaction) is a new spell, not a Heighten entry.
19. **Proposed spells in `docs/analysis/spells/schools/*.md` are stale drafts** — they predate current design directions and may contain errors and false assumptions. Never treat them as canon or "already designed"; override with fresh designs that follow the current principles.
20. **Material cost only when coins actually burn** — the *material cost (X)* property (coin value) appears only when a cast literally consumes coins/coin-valued goods. A reusable focus (mirror, bone set) is *material (X)*, not a cost, and carries no coin value. Coin amounts follow the rank table in rank-scaling.md (anchored to spell scroll costs: consumed ≈ one tier below same-rank scroll; R5 = 5,000+). Most spells consume nothing.
21. **A classic name obligates the classic's core action** — if a spell borrows an iconic name (Knock, Identify, Sending), its effect must actually deliver that spell's defining function (Knock *opens*, not merely "grants boons to open"). Assist-only reskins keep an original name; classic names carry expectations.
22. **Don't invent rules terms inside a spell — reference existing rules, or add the missing rules first** — if a spell needs a classification the rules don't define (e.g. "masterwork/complex lock"), that's a signal to add the small rules section (locks got a Quality/cost/Difficulty table in gear.md) and then reference its established terms. When no rules exist and none are warranted, phrase the effect in plain in-world language instead of implying a mechanical category. **This includes creature categories**: a spell that targets or detects by creature nature must use the canonical **creature types** from `docs/08-creatures/02-creature-rules.md` (Aberration, Automaton, Beast, Draconic, Giant, Humanoid, Monstrosity, Ooze, Plant, Spirit, Undead — plus their listed subtypes, e.g. Ethereal Undead), never invented flavor labels like "daimon", "incorporeal creatures", or "otherworldly beings". (Owner caught "daimon/incorporeal" on Sense Spirits — corrected to Spirit + Undead.)
23. **No semicolons or em/en dashes in game content** — the game uses natural, direct language. Split into separate sentences, or use "such as"/"and". (Applies to published effect/flavor text; scratch design notes are exempt.)
24. **A flat single-success effect is often the correct choice, especially for utility** — not every spell needs Weak/Strong/Critical tiers. When a spell's value is binary (a door is locked or it isn't, you understand a language or you don't), give it one effect that happens on any success. Predictability is a feature: the player knows a successful cast works as intended. Only add SL tiers when there is a genuine *potency* axis to scale (damage, healing, duration of a condition, amount of information divined). Reserve this for effects where "more" is meaningful.
25. **Optional modifiers belong on Heighten, never on Success Level** — a binary feature that is unrelated to how well you cast (a hidden sigil, a damage backlash, an added sense) must never be gated behind Strong/Critical, because SL is not the player's choice and such features are not "potency." If a spell should offer such a modifier, make it a Heighten option the player actively pays for and chooses. This is the corollary to principle 2: SL scales potency, Heighten grants options.
26. **Heighten cascades to R5 by default, unless the scaling axis is genuinely exhausted** — a Heighten entry normally provides a benefit at every rank above the base up to rank 5. **Use the published house idiom: explicit per-rank lines, one per rank, each phrased "…instead"** — `(Rank 3) … instead. (Rank 4) … instead. (Rank 5) … instead.` Do NOT use the "For each additional rank of Focus spent, …" formula phrasing; it reads clunky and does not match any published spell (write out each rank's line). For a menu-style Heighten, escalate the count of choices per rank: `(Rank 3) choose one of the benefits below. (Rank 4) choose two instead. (Rank 5) choose three instead.` Spells that merely stop at R3/R4 out of old habit are legacy and should be extended when touched. **Exception**: some effects have a natural ceiling the scaling reaches before R5 (e.g. a timeframe that reaches "the object's entire history" — there is nowhere further to scale). Then the Heighten legitimately caps at that rank. Do NOT pad it to R5 by bolting on an unrelated effect just to fill ranks — that violates principle 25. A limited, honest cascade beats a padded one. (Psychometry's Legend Lore heighten caps at R4 for exactly this reason.)
27. **Validate a new spell against talents and other subsystems, not just other spells** — before finalizing, check whether a skill talent, folk trait, or item already covers the same job (e.g. Identify vs the *Identify Artifact* Lore talent, detect-magic vs *Magical Sense*). A spell must not strictly outclass a dedicated talent that a player spent a slot on. Match its power and copy its constraints (Quality-scaled difficulty, once-per-target, fail consequences) so the two sit in parity, with each keeping a distinct edge (the talent is repeatable and upgrades; the spell needs no slot but costs Focus). Grep the talent files, not only the spell files.
28. **Ground info-gathering tiers in the real anatomy of what is examined** — a spell (or talent) that reveals information must scale along the axis the target actually has, per the established rules — never a borrowed D&D assumption. A Nexus magic item carries exactly one enchantment (rare artifacts excepted) with Quality-scaled values, so a "one property → all properties" ladder is wrong (both collapse to the same reveal); the honest ladder is general category → the enchantment in full → cursed status/hidden property + any extra enchantments. Curses are a defined subsystem (curses.mdx), so a curse reveal is grounded, split it as Strong "a curse exists" / Critical "exactly how it works." Read the target subsystem's rules before writing the tiers; when a spell exposes a mismatch, fix the spell and the old ability together.
29. **A GM-facing effect must give the GM parameters to move within, never open-ended "the GM decides"** — Nexus deliberately does not leave the GM to invent a resolution on the spot for something they may not have prepared. Whenever a spell's effect hands a decision to the GM (what a divination reveals, what an omen says, what an insight contains), spell out a concrete menu of answer types, categories, or bounded options the GM chooses among. Include a safety-valve option for when nothing concrete applies (e.g. a symbolic omen). This keeps play flowing and never forces on-the-spot creativity. Models: *Divination* (four defined answer types, GM picks), the *Magical Sense* talent (pick a category, GM fills it), Psychometry's Discovery. Contrast the D&D idiom ("the DM offers a truthful reply") which this house style rejects.
30. **Refer to a creature or target as "they/their/them," never "it/its"** — Nexus canon uses the singular *they* for any creature, target, ally, or enemy in rules text, even a single one. Reserve "it/its" for objects, zones, spells, areas, and other non-creatures (a door, the slippery area, the zone, a *Wall of Force*). When a spell effect refers back to "the target," "a creature," or "the attacker," continue with they/their/them ("the target grows one Size larger, their melee attacks deal +2 damage, and they gain +1 boon"). This is a wording rule, not a mechanical one, but the owner enforces it on every published line.
31. **Never scale a spell's tier cap on Heighten** — many control and removal spells cap their targets by tier (affect creatures of a tier up to your Mysticism/Arcana) to respect the mortal ceiling and stop the spell trivializing bosses. That cap is a fixed guardrail, not a scaling axis. Heightening it ("tier up to Mysticism + 1, + 2") reads as an escalating auto-win against ever stronger foes, which the owner rejects. Keep the tier cap constant at every rank and scale the honest axes instead, duration, area, or number of targets. (Sleep, Banishment, Serene Exile all had tier-scaling heightens cut for exactly this.)
32. **Every Heighten rank must be self-contained, restating all inherited effects, not just the delta** — in a cumulative cascade a reader who casts at rank N reads only that rank's line, so it must list the full state at that rank, not the one thing that changed since rank N-1. Wrong: "(R3) lasts a medium duration. (R4) also covers a short area" (a reader could miss that R4 still includes the medium duration). Right: "(R3) lasts a medium duration instead. (R4) lasts a medium duration and covers a short area instead." This is why the "…instead" idiom works, each line replaces the base wholesale. Only genuinely independent single-rank heightens (one line, nothing to inherit) are exempt.
33. **Weigh a spell's worldbuilding implications, not just its combat math** — Nexus is Bronze Age sword & sorcery: magic is rare, feared, priest- or sorcerer-gated, never institutionalized. Before finalizing any spell with social, economic, legal, informational, or logistical reach (truth-compulsion, scrying, sending, resurrection, food/water creation, healing, teleportation), ask what happens if it becomes a routine tool of courts, markets, armies, or rulers. A spell must not modernize or industrialize the setting (no CSI courtrooms, no magical telegraph networks, no death made trivial). Three levers keep a spell genre-safe: keep it **rare and costly** (rank, Focus, concentrate, mystic/arcane gating, material cost), make it **constrain rather than extract or auto-solve** (Zone of Truth forbids lies but cannot force answers, so intrigue survives), and leave **in-world counterplay** (wards, resistance, evasion, refusal, social workarounds). When a spell's reach is setting-shaping, add a short GM/worldbuilding framing note (a temple truth-rite, not a courtroom gadget) rather than pretending the implication away. Evaluate this as an explicit balance axis alongside power and theme.
34. **Use the house micro-conventions for common rules phrasings** — match the exact idioms published rules already use rather than paraphrasing. The floor on a reduction is written "(min. 0)" in parentheses, not "to a minimum of 0" (e.g. "They lose 1 AV (min. 0)"). A stat penalty on an attack is written as a signed value, "-2 damage with Strength-based attacks," not "2 less damage with attacks based on Strength." An in-effect roll against a fixed number is written "Roll vs. TN 16," not "vs. legendary difficulty (16)" (the labelled form like "Medium (8)" belongs in the Target column of the mini-table, not in the prose). A bane/boon is written as a signed count that "suffers"/"gains": "Ranged attacks through the area suffer +1 bane," never "are made with a bane" or "with disadvantage." When unsure how to phrase a recurring mechanic (a floor, a duration, a die-step change, a per-scene limit), grep an existing spell or rules page for the pattern and copy it verbatim.
35. **A conjured barrier, wall, cage, or construct must have concrete durability stats and never be impregnable** — any spell that creates a physical or force structure (wall, cage, dome, barrier, sealed box) states Parry, Dodge, AV, and HP so it can be attacked and destroyed like an object from either side, giving trapped or blocked creatures real counterplay over a few rounds. Never write "cannot be harmed by nonmagical means" or an unbreakable construct. Use *Wall of Force* as the baseline (R3: 10 Parry, 10 Dodge, 8 AV, 40 HP) and scale up with rank (Force Cage R4: 12/12, 10 AV, 60 HP). A single dedicated counter (a *Disintegrate*-class force-unmaking, or a dispel) may still end it outright, but the plain stat block is the default counterplay.
36. **Synergies are diegetic, never rotational — this is a TTRPG, not an MMORPG** — the synergy framework (synergy-framework.md: setup/payoff/extender, cold-cast deficit, shared sockets) is design vocabulary only, never in-game keywords or stack counters. Every combo state must be something the table can see and describe in the fiction (a creature on fire, prone, terrified; slick ground; a hanging ward) and must make sense in the campaign world outside combat too (extends principle 33). If a combo only reads as an ability-rotation optimization, redesign it. (Owner set this boundary when approving the framework.)
37. **No cheap magical safety nets — prefer disturbance-triggered saves over auto-protection** — when guarding a control spell against instant-kill exploits (the Polymorph kill-box: transform, carry the helpless beast to a pit, release), do not bolt on protective magic that saves the victim from the environment on reversion ("reverts to the nearest safe space" feels cheap and unearned). Instead make the exploit *hard and risky*: any physical disturbance of the controlled creature (grabbing, carrying, shoving, damage) grants an immediate re-roll of their save, mirroring Sleep's wake-on-disturbance. The kill-box then remains as a legitimate high-risk strategy with payoff, not a cheap trick — every step of the setup invites the spell to break. (Owner set this resolving Polymorph Others; Sleep is the model.)
38. **If a spell's exploits trace to a reverting physical state, remove the reversion rather than patching edge cases** — when a spell creates a temporary change to the physical world that later undoes itself (a hole that reseals, a shape that reverts, a wall that reforms), the reversion is an infinite edge-case generator: every object, creature, or position caught in the change at the moment it reverts is a new exploit (entombment, kinetic harvest, hold-open, trapped loot, bisection). You cannot patch your way out — each fix spawns the next case, because the exploitable core IS the reverting state. The elegant fix is to eliminate the reversion from first principles: make the change permanent (Part Stone bores a permanent passage — no reseal, so no reseal cases can even be stated), or make it never alter matter at all (a permeability field creatures pass through, so no object can be caught in it). Balance the now-permanent effect through its preconditions (material, thickness, ritual casting, warding immunity) instead of through the reversion. When neither elegant form works and the spell stays exploitable, cut it rather than shipping a patched-but-leaky version. (Owner established this resolving Passwall after the reseal spawned a chain of exploits — kinetic push-out, hold-open, sub-thickness entombment — each fix exposing the next.)
39. **Teleportation and long-form travel rank one tier higher in Nexus than their D&D equivalent** — magical transit (teleport, tree stride, planar travel, gate) is deliberately rarer and more special in Nexus's grounded Bronze-Age setting than in high-magic D&D, where such magic is commonplace. When a travel/teleport concept straddles a rank boundary, resolve upward: Tree Stride (D&D lvl 5 → would-be R3) sits at R4, and Teleportation Circle likewise landed at R4. Judge these by in-world rarity, not raw D&D-level math. (Owner set this confirming Tree Stride at R4.)
40. **All spellcasting requires a verbal component — speaking words of power aloud** — this is the assumed default across the system (the *Silent Spell* metamagic confirms it: it costs +1 Focus to swap the spoken words for subtle gestures and is explicitly what "allows you to cast a spell while being silenced"). Consequences a designer can rely on: a silenced, gagged, or mute caster cannot cast normally; any effect that suppresses sound (Silent Night, a silence zone) disables spellcasting inside it. **But the block is not absolute** — a caster using silent casting (the *Silent Spell* metamagic, or any future gestural equivalent) bypasses it. Word anti-cast silence effects as "no spell can be cast within the zone unless cast by silent means (such as the *Silent Spell* metamagic)," never as an unconditional lockout. (Owner ruled this designing Silent Night; *Silent Spell* is the canonical interaction.)
41. **Physical is the default damage type — never write "physical damage," just "damage"** — bludgeoning/slashing/piercing physical damage is the unstated default, reduced by AV normally. Name a type in spell text only when it is *not* physical (fire, frost, lightning, acid, radiant, necrotic, blast, psychic, poison). Write "deal +6 damage," "5 damage (ignore AV)" — not "+6 physical damage." Matches published spell phrasing. (Owner set this reviewing the stub batch.)
42. **Placed-hazard zones cast against a fixed TN; the entering creature then rolls its save vs. your Resist.** Distinguish two AoE shapes. (a) An *aimed* AoE the caster delivers this turn (cone, burst, called bolts) uses `vs. Dodge`/`vs. Resist` in the Target column and resolves on the *caster's* roll result — the caster's success level tiers the damage (Pyroclasm, Cone of Cold, Thunder Clap). (b) A *placed* hazard zone that persists and catches whoever enters or lingers (Sandstorm, Distortion Field, Earthquake, Reverse Gravity, Fungal Growth) is **not** targeted at a creature at cast time — it uses a **fixed TN in the Target column** (`6 + 2×rank`: R3 = Very Hard 12, R4 = Extreme 14, matching published Fungal Growth) and resolves "On a success, you create the zone…". Because there is no caster attack roll against a target, there is **no caster success level** — so the recurring hazard is **flat, not SL-tiered** (see principle 44). After the fixed-TN cast, resolve who the zone catches in one of **two** valid ways — pick by feel, don't mix: (b1) **creature-rolls-its-save**, phrased "must succeed on an [Attribute] + [Skill] roll vs. your Resist or [effect]" (Fungal Growth, Distortion Field, Reverse Gravity); pick the attribute+skill from the fiction of enduring the hazard (falling/force: Agility + Athletics). (b2) **caster-result-vs-defense**, phrased "compare your casting result to the [Parry/Dodge/Resist] of each creature… each creature you meet or beat [effect]" — the single casting roll is reused each turn, no per-turn action or creature save (Nightmare Realm, Insect Swarm, Zone of Truth, Earthquake, Storm Cloud, Cyclone, Lightning Storm). Prefer b2 for a recurring per-turn hazard (one roll drives the whole duration, less clutter); prefer b1 for a one-shot-on-entry check. Either way the Target column holds only the **fixed cast TN**. **Deprecated:** the older "vs. this spell's target Difficulty" phrasing (Web, Grease), and putting `vs. Resist` in the Target column of a placed zone. (Owner confirmed fixed-TN-cast on Sandstorm, Distortion Field, Reverse Gravity; switched Earthquake to the b2 casting-result-vs-Parry pattern.)
43. **Falling damage (and other general-rule damage) is never a spell stat — defer to the rule** — falling damage is a fixed distance-based table (`docs/05-combat/03-distances-movement.md`: Close 5 / Short 10 / Medium 20 / Long 40 / Very Long 60 / Extreme 80, ignores AV, causes prone). A spell that makes a creature fall (Reverse Gravity, a knockback off a height, a collapsing surface) causes the fall and lets the general rule set the damage — it does **not** invent its own SL falling damage. Write "takes falling damage for the distance fallen." Same for any effect the core rules already price (drowning, environmental hazards): reference the rule, don't restate or re-scale it. Such a spell often has no SL damage line at all — it is a flat control effect on any success. (Owner set this on Reverse Gravity.)
44. **Recurring zone/aura damage is flat, scaled by rank — never Weak/Strong/Critical.** A persistent zone that deals damage each turn (Wind Ward, Sandstorm, Earthquake, Distortion Field) resolves its cast as pass/fail ("On a success…") and deals a **fixed** per-tick number, scaled only by rank or Heighten — never split into SL tiers. Two reasons: placed zones have no caster success level to tier from (principle 42), and a recurring effect multiplied across many turns and targets makes SL swings explosive. Benchmark ladder from published Wind Ward (R2 +2 / R3 +4): roughly **+2 per rank** for a **utility/ward** zone whose value is spread across several effects. A **damage-primary** zone (Sandstorm R4 = 8) runs a step hotter, since its soft control doesn't carry the spell; a **control-primary** zone (Earthquake R5 = 8, a step under a same-rank damage-primary's ~10) sits lower, since repeating hard control (prone, fissures, collapse) carries it. So weight the tick by how much the zone's non-damage layer contributes, don't apply +2/rank blindly. Non-attack scouring/debris damage ignores AV. Aimed per-turn attacks are the exception — a spell that compares a casting result to each creature's Dodge each turn (Storm Cloud, Cyclone, Lightning Storm) legitimately produces an SL that tiers its per-hit damage. (Owner confirmed on Sandstorm, Earthquake, Distortion Field.)
45. **The range distance in "in <distance> range of the target location" IS the zone radius — never also state a separate area size.** "a medium area in short range of the target location" double-specifies the size and is contradictory. Pick one: the canonical radius form "in <short/medium/…> range of the target location" (the distance = the radius, per rank-scaling.md), or the other AoE idiom "choose a <close/…> area within range". Not both. (Owner flagged on Sandstorm; also fixed Earthquake, Lightning Storm.)
46. **Sight-blocking zones use the defined area properties Lightly Obscured / Heavily Obscured — never an ad-hoc gloss.** These are canonical area properties on `docs/05-combat/03-distances-movement.md` (Lightly Obscured = +1 bane on sight rolls in/through the area; Heavily Obscured = blinded for seeing into/through/within). Fog/smoke/sand/dust spells reference them by name, exactly as Difficult Terrain is referenced — do not re-explain the mechanical effect inline. Dim Light / Total Darkness remain the light-based parallels. (Owner added these properties when Sandstorm needed a mechanical "heavily obscured".)
47. **Every condition/effect duration must be one of the defined time intervals — never an improvised span like "until the start of their next turn".** Nexus uses a fixed ladder (`docs/06-scenes/02-effect-durations.md`), the shortest being **briefly** (a ~1-turn effect). Ad-hoc phrasings such as "until the start of their next turn", "for 1 round", or "until they leave" are invalid — use **briefly** for the shortest, then short/medium/long/very long. If the effect you want is "roughly one turn," it is **briefly**. (Owner flagged "slowed until the start of their next turn" on Sandstorm.)
48. **An in-effect attack/skill roll must name the school's own casting skill — Mysticism for mystic traditions, Arcana for arcane disciplines.** When a spell's text calls for a roll by the caster (a bolt, a hurled object, a repeated attack), it uses the school's skill: mystic (Spirit + Mysticism), arcane (Mind + Arcana). Never write an Arcana roll into a mystic spell or vice versa. Sanity-check every draft against its school before finalizing. (Owner caught a Mysticism-school Lightning Storm rolling Arcana.)
49. **A weapon-empower spell that swaps in spell power uses the terse idiom "use your spell power instead of the attack's base damage."** Base damage is the defined quantity — ½ the attribute you rolled the attack with (`docs/05-combat/02-attacking.md`), which for a spell rolled with Spirit/Mind already equals your spell power. So the swap is precise and near-neutral (it lets a low-Strength priest key weapon damage off their casting attribute). Do NOT write the verbose "instead of the attribute you would use for the weapon (Strength/Agility)". (Owner standardized on Mighty Strike, Oaken Strike, Glorious Strike.)
50. **A spell that grants or rides a full weapon attack adds bonus damage at the half/multi-target column for its rank, not the single-target column.** The weapon attack itself already supplies roughly single-target-spell-equivalent damage (base ½-attribute + weapon damage ×SL-multiplier 1/2/3), so a full single-target rider double-dips — at R3 a full +6/12/18 rider beat a pure R3 nuke at every tier *and* still kept the weapon damage. Use the half column (R3 = +4/8/12). This is distinct from the two other weapon-spell budgets: **enchant-weapon** spells run a small sustained flat rider (+2 → +4/+6 per hit over a duration), and **instant-strike** spells (Mighty/Oaken Strike) add no bonus at all (pure spell-power-for-base-damage swap). 51. **At-will (R0) spells never trigger Durability checks or any other attrition subsystem** — an unlimited-use cantrip with an equipment-degradation rider grinds gear to dust for free (spam every turn, no Focus cost). R0 riders stay weak and static (Acid Splash's "ignore 1/2 AV" is the model — intentional, keep it). Durability pressure, supply drain, and similar attrition riders start at Focus-costed ranks (R1+), where each cast has a real price. This is the attrition sibling of principle 11 (no R0 Quick Action healing). (Owner rejected the proposed Acid Splash durability rework at R0 for exactly this.)
52. **Reference subsystems by their current terms — sweep for renamed mechanics when touching a spell** — rules terminology evolves ("encumbrance limit" became **carrying capacity**, 1/2 Strength + 8, `docs/04-equipment/01-items.md`). A spell referencing a stale term is a bug: verify every referenced mechanic still exists under that name, and when a rename is found, grep all spell files for the old term and fix every instance in the same pass. (Owner caught "encumbrance limit" on Strong Telekinesis; Weak Telekinesis had the same issue.)
53. **A defined keyword is referenced by name only — never re-explain its rules in spell text** — area properties (Constricted, Difficult Terrain, Heavily Obscured), conditions, and other defined game terms are their own single source of truth; a spell just names them ("the gap is a **Constricted** area"), exactly as principle 46 established for the Obscured properties. Restating the keyword's effects inline creates drift when the definition changes and defeats the point of defining it. Principle 14's verbatim-repetition rule applies only to shared limiting *sentences* that have no keyword of their own (the Wound-healing cap) — once a rule is promoted to a defined keyword/property, reference replaces repetition. (Owner corrected an inline Constricted re-explanation on Stone Pillar.)

# Designer Principles — Spell Design

Distilled from owner feedback — **binding rules, not suggestions**. Read before designing any spell. When the owner corrects or refines a design decision in session, append it here (numbered, one line, with the reasoning).

1. **Spells assist, never bypass** — travel/social/exploration spells grant boons and reduce difficulty; they never auto-succeed skill challenges or trivialize game modes.
2. **SL scales magnitude, not effect type** — the primary utility/control effect (condition, duration, target count) is identical on any success. Only damage, healing, and *secondary* effects scale with Success Level. A control spell applying "dazed" on Weak still applies "dazed" on Critical — the Critical adds bonus damage or a secondary debuff.
3. **Heighten, don't duplicate** — reserve new spell entries for genuinely different concepts.
4. **Standardized reactive defense** — every school's R1 Quick Action reactive spell has the same base ("+2 to Dodge or Parry against the triggering attack"), no SL scaling, plus one school-specific secondary effect.
5. **R5 material costs match the item economy** — 5,000+ coins (Q6–Q7 tier), never trivial amounts.
6. **Traits are thematic seeds, not literal requirements** — an R0 "teleportation" cantrip means spatial manipulation (interact at range, sense space), not actual teleportation. Interpret traits creatively at each power level.
7. **Arcane defiles, Mystic honors** — even on shared thematic territory (Necromancy vs. Death), philosophies are opposed. Never share spells between philosophically opposed schools. (Genuinely neutral spells between compatible schools can be shared — see per-school notes, e.g. Acid Splash between Evocation and Nature.)
8. **Binding means containment** — Conjuration's binding trait is lasting constructs (wards, cages, dimensional anchors), not movement debuffs.
9. **Every school needs internal completeness** — R1 reactive Quick Action spell, defensive options, utility/non-combat effects, some damage capability, a repeating condition/gimmick identity, and setup+payoff synergies.
10. **AoE trades damage for targets** — half single-target spell bonus at R2+ (R0–R1 keep legacy values). AoE pays off at 3+ clustered targets (150% throughput), never strictly beats focused fire.
11. **No R0 Quick Action healing** — Quick Action healing starts at R1. At-will, action-free healing would trivialize attrition; healing always costs Focus or an Action.
12. **Limits must live in the fiction, never in game structure** — never scope an effect to meta-constructs ("only during encounter turns", "only in combat"). Constrain power through diegetic costs the world can see: concentration, Focus, Actions, duration, positioning, material costs, or daily rhythms. If an effect is too strong outside combat, change its magnitude or duration — don't bolt on a combat-only gate.
13. **Wound healing gates at R4+ for new spells** — Wounds are serious injuries; recovering them magically is a major effect. Existing lower-rank exceptions (Healing Touch R1 ritual-only, Abundance of Life R3) are grandfathered because their restrictions are intense (extended casting / once per day per creature + Action cost). Don't add new wound healing below R4.
14. **Shared rules must be visible at the point of use — never buried in reference pages** — a global rule hidden in a rules chapter gets forgotten at the table. Default for a shared limiting mechanism: repeat the **same short sentence verbatim** in every affected spell (current example: "A creature can only benefit from one Wound-healing spell effect per day."). A **spell property keyword** (the *illusory* model) is the escalation path only when the family grows large enough that verbatim repetition becomes a maintenance burden — and note a property still hides the rule one lookup away, so the bar is high (owner call).
15. **Effect text order: general application first, exceptions after** — declare the basic effect completely, then follow with its restrictions/exceptions as separate sentences. Never interleave a limit into the middle of the effect declaration.
16. **HP is encounter-scale only** — HP refills at any short break; it never interacts with rest-scale or travel-scale effects. Night/travel effects trade in the long-term trackers: Fatigue, Wounds, Resolve, Focus. "Regain HP at dawn" is a nonsense effect.
17. **Fatigue removal must respect travel attrition** — nightly recovery baseline is 1 Fatigue; travel inflicts ~0–2/day. A spell granting +1 removal already doubles recovery (acceptable ceiling); never scale removal higher via Heighten or rank, or fatigue accumulation stops threatening entirely.
18. **Heighten upscales, never transforms** — Heighten entries may only increase existing magnitudes (damage, healing, target count, range, duration, reach/timeframe). A genuinely new capability (new mode of use, new sense, new interaction) is a new spell, not a Heighten entry.
19. **Proposed spells in `docs/analysis/spells/schools/*.md` are stale drafts** — they predate current design directions and may contain errors and false assumptions. Never treat them as canon or "already designed"; override with fresh designs that follow the current principles.
20. **Material cost only when coins actually burn** — the *material cost (X)* property (coin value) appears only when a cast literally consumes coins/coin-valued goods. A reusable focus (mirror, bone set) is *material (X)*, not a cost, and carries no coin value. Coin amounts follow the rank table in rank-scaling.md (anchored to spell scroll costs: consumed ≈ one tier below same-rank scroll; R5 = 5,000+). Most spells consume nothing.
21. **A classic name obligates the classic's core action** — if a spell borrows an iconic name (Knock, Identify, Sending), its effect must actually deliver that spell's defining function (Knock *opens*, not merely "grants boons to open"). Assist-only reskins keep an original name; classic names carry expectations.
22. **Don't invent rules terms inside a spell — reference existing rules, or add the missing rules first** — if a spell needs a classification the rules don't define (e.g. "masterwork/complex lock"), that's a signal to add the small rules section (locks got a Quality/cost/Difficulty table in gear.md) and then reference its established terms. When no rules exist and none are warranted, phrase the effect in plain in-world language instead of implying a mechanical category.
23. **No semicolons or em/en dashes in game content** — the game uses natural, direct language. Split into separate sentences, or use "such as"/"and". (Applies to published effect/flavor text; scratch design notes are exempt.)
24. **A flat single-success effect is often the correct choice, especially for utility** — not every spell needs Weak/Strong/Critical tiers. When a spell's value is binary (a door is locked or it isn't, you understand a language or you don't), give it one effect that happens on any success. Predictability is a feature: the player knows a successful cast works as intended. Only add SL tiers when there is a genuine *potency* axis to scale (damage, healing, duration of a condition, amount of information divined). Reserve this for effects where "more" is meaningful.
25. **Optional modifiers belong on Heighten, never on Success Level** — a binary feature that is unrelated to how well you cast (a hidden sigil, a damage backlash, an added sense) must never be gated behind Strong/Critical, because SL is not the player's choice and such features are not "potency." If a spell should offer such a modifier, make it a Heighten option the player actively pays for and chooses. This is the corollary to principle 2: SL scales potency, Heighten grants options.
26. **Heighten cascades to R5 by default, unless the scaling axis is genuinely exhausted** — a Heighten entry normally provides a benefit at every rank above the base up to rank 5. **Use the published house idiom: explicit per-rank lines, one per rank, each phrased "…instead"** — `(Rank 3) … instead. (Rank 4) … instead. (Rank 5) … instead.` Do NOT use the "For each additional rank of Focus spent, …" formula phrasing; it reads clunky and does not match any published spell (write out each rank's line). For a menu-style Heighten, escalate the count of choices per rank: `(Rank 3) choose one of the benefits below. (Rank 4) choose two instead. (Rank 5) choose three instead.` Spells that merely stop at R3/R4 out of old habit are legacy and should be extended when touched. **Exception**: some effects have a natural ceiling the scaling reaches before R5 (e.g. a timeframe that reaches "the object's entire history" — there is nowhere further to scale). Then the Heighten legitimately caps at that rank. Do NOT pad it to R5 by bolting on an unrelated effect just to fill ranks — that violates principle 25. A limited, honest cascade beats a padded one. (Psychometry's Legend Lore heighten caps at R4 for exactly this reason.)
27. **Validate a new spell against talents and other subsystems, not just other spells** — before finalizing, check whether a skill talent, folk trait, or item already covers the same job (e.g. Identify vs the *Identify Artifact* Lore talent, detect-magic vs *Magical Sense*). A spell must not strictly outclass a dedicated talent that a player spent a slot on. Match its power and copy its constraints (Quality-scaled difficulty, once-per-target, fail consequences) so the two sit in parity, with each keeping a distinct edge (the talent is repeatable and upgrades; the spell needs no slot but costs Focus). Grep the talent files, not only the spell files.
28. **Ground info-gathering tiers in the real anatomy of what is examined** — a spell (or talent) that reveals information must scale along the axis the target actually has, per the established rules — never a borrowed D&D assumption. A Nexus magic item carries exactly one enchantment (rare artifacts excepted) with Quality-scaled values, so a "one property → all properties" ladder is wrong (both collapse to the same reveal); the honest ladder is general category → the enchantment in full → cursed status/hidden property + any extra enchantments. Curses are a defined subsystem (curses.mdx), so a curse reveal is grounded, split it as Strong "a curse exists" / Critical "exactly how it works." Read the target subsystem's rules before writing the tiers; when a spell exposes a mismatch, fix the spell and the old ability together.
29. **A GM-facing effect must give the GM parameters to move within, never open-ended "the GM decides"** — Nexus deliberately does not leave the GM to invent a resolution on the spot for something they may not have prepared. Whenever a spell's effect hands a decision to the GM (what a divination reveals, what an omen says, what an insight contains), spell out a concrete menu of answer types, categories, or bounded options the GM chooses among. Include a safety-valve option for when nothing concrete applies (e.g. a symbolic omen). This keeps play flowing and never forces on-the-spot creativity. Models: *Divination* (four defined answer types, GM picks), the *Magical Sense* talent (pick a category, GM fills it), Psychometry's Discovery. Contrast the D&D idiom ("the DM offers a truthful reply") which this house style rejects.
30. **Refer to a creature or target as "they/their/them," never "it/its"** — Nexus canon uses the singular *they* for any creature, target, ally, or enemy in rules text, even a single one. Reserve "it/its" for objects, zones, spells, areas, and other non-creatures (a door, the slippery area, the zone, a *Wall of Force*). When a spell effect refers back to "the target," "a creature," or "the attacker," continue with they/their/them ("the target grows one Size larger, their melee attacks deal +2 damage, and they gain +1 boon"). This is a wording rule, not a mechanical one, but the owner enforces it on every published line.
31. **Never scale a spell's tier cap on Heighten** — many control and removal spells cap their targets by tier (affect creatures of a tier up to your Mysticism/Arcana) to respect the mortal ceiling and stop the spell trivializing bosses. That cap is a fixed guardrail, not a scaling axis. Heightening it ("tier up to Mysticism + 1, + 2") reads as an escalating auto-win against ever stronger foes, which the owner rejects. Keep the tier cap constant at every rank and scale the honest axes instead, duration, area, or number of targets. (Sleep, Banishment, Serene Exile all had tier-scaling heightens cut for exactly this.)
32. **Every Heighten rank must be self-contained, restating all inherited effects, not just the delta** — in a cumulative cascade a reader who casts at rank N reads only that rank's line, so it must list the full state at that rank, not the one thing that changed since rank N-1. Wrong: "(R3) lasts a medium duration. (R4) also covers a short area" (a reader could miss that R4 still includes the medium duration). Right: "(R3) lasts a medium duration instead. (R4) lasts a medium duration and covers a short area instead." This is why the "…instead" idiom works, each line replaces the base wholesale. Only genuinely independent single-rank heightens (one line, nothing to inherit) are exempt.
33. **Weigh a spell's worldbuilding implications, not just its combat math** — Nexus is Bronze Age sword & sorcery: magic is rare, feared, priest- or sorcerer-gated, never institutionalized. Before finalizing any spell with social, economic, legal, informational, or logistical reach (truth-compulsion, scrying, sending, resurrection, food/water creation, healing, teleportation), ask what happens if it becomes a routine tool of courts, markets, armies, or rulers. A spell must not modernize or industrialize the setting (no CSI courtrooms, no magical telegraph networks, no death made trivial). Three levers keep a spell genre-safe: keep it **rare and costly** (rank, Focus, concentrate, mystic/arcane gating, material cost), make it **constrain rather than extract or auto-solve** (Zone of Truth forbids lies but cannot force answers, so intrigue survives), and leave **in-world counterplay** (wards, resistance, evasion, refusal, social workarounds). When a spell's reach is setting-shaping, add a short GM/worldbuilding framing note (a temple truth-rite, not a courtroom gadget) rather than pretending the implication away. Evaluate this as an explicit balance axis alongside power and theme.
34. **Use the house micro-conventions for common rules phrasings** — match the exact idioms published rules already use rather than paraphrasing. The floor on a reduction is written "(min. 0)" in parentheses, not "to a minimum of 0" (e.g. "They lose 1 AV (min. 0)"). A stat penalty on an attack is written as a signed value, "-2 damage with Strength-based attacks," not "2 less damage with attacks based on Strength." An in-effect roll against a fixed number is written "Roll vs. TN 16," not "vs. legendary difficulty (16)" (the labelled form like "Medium (8)" belongs in the Target column of the mini-table, not in the prose). When unsure how to phrase a recurring mechanic (a floor, a duration, a die-step change, a per-scene limit), grep an existing spell or rules page for the pattern and copy it verbatim.
35. **A conjured barrier, wall, cage, or construct must have concrete durability stats and never be impregnable** — any spell that creates a physical or force structure (wall, cage, dome, barrier, sealed box) states Parry, Dodge, AV, and HP so it can be attacked and destroyed like an object from either side, giving trapped or blocked creatures real counterplay over a few rounds. Never write "cannot be harmed by nonmagical means" or an unbreakable construct. Use *Wall of Force* as the baseline (R3: 10 Parry, 10 Dodge, 8 AV, 40 HP) and scale up with rank (Force Cage R4: 12/12, 10 AV, 60 HP). A single dedicated counter (a *Disintegrate*-class force-unmaking, or a dispel) may still end it outright, but the plain stat block is the default counterplay.
